CyberSecurity

What does it do?

Cybersecurity is defined as the practices that are taken to protect critical IT systems as well as sensitive information from digital threats, such as hacking, phishing, scams, etc. As the technological world is constantly evolving every day, so does the risk of attack. Loads of information are being uploaded on the internet, which may be prone to unauthorized users performing data breaches to gain access to these information.

Some popular software security risks may include:

Viruses - a type of malicious software that is designed to essentially hijack a series of computer systems by replicating itself and spreading. They often operate by acting as a legitimate piece of application when executed will have unexpected and damaging effects to the system, such as corrupting/destroying data, freezing the computer, and many more.

Ransomware - similar to viruses, ransomware is another type of malicious software designed to hijack computer systems. When a ransomware attack is performed on a system, it essentially locks up the files stored in that computer, in order to unlock the files, a “ransom” is required to be paid within a certain time limit or else the files will be permanently deleted. In 2021, approximately more than 500 million ransomware attacks were performed, costing around USD$20 billion in damages.

Phishing scams - are a way that cybercriminals can steal private, sensitive information (login credentials, banking details, etc.) from unsuspecting victims. Scams of this nature are usually done through emails/messages that pretend to be from a large organization (such as a bank, police, utility companies) to make it more believable. It can be easy to identify phishing emails as spelling errors, formatting errors may be present which would not be on a genuine email. If you do not know whether an email is real or a scam, the best course of action would be to physically contact the organization who seemingly sent the message in order to confirm with them.

Distributed Denial of Service (DDOS) Attack - is a malicious attempt to disrupt internet traffic, through overwhelming it with networks of Internet-connected machines. In real world terms, a DDOS Attack can be comparable to hundreds of cars intentionally causing a traffic jam on a highway. A victim’s IP address can be exposed and targeted by a botnet and sends lots of requests to that respective IP, causing the server to crash. A notorious example of a DDOS attack was when an internet alias named “MafiaBoy” brought down the biggest websites (Amazon, Yahoo, eBay) in February, 2000. Overall, the attack caused over $1 billion in damages internationally.

Some ways to protect from security risks is to invest in cybersecurity measures such as:

Anti Viruses - such as Windows Defender, Norton Security, McAfee, etc. can help protect your computer against malware by providing real-time protection through constantly scanning files stored on the system, and removing any suspicious software.

Multi factor authentication - where a user cannot access a certain application/software with just the login credentials alone, they will need multiple other pieces of evidence that prove that they are the true owner of that account. This can be done through sending a PIN code to a mobile number or email, asking security questions that only the owner will know, or even using an authenticator app such as Google which generates verification codes every few minutes to avoid unauthorized logins.

What is the likely impact?

As technology is becoming more and more relevant and integrated into our lives, we can often find ourselves being overly dependent on it, transacting with banks online, storing sensitive information (such as login credentials to various sites) in password managers and many more. However, while it may make our lives easier, it can also leave our information vulnerable to risks and threats. Therefore, it is important to make cybersecurity one’s top priority when interacting with anything online.

Nowadays, a lack of awareness about the internet is oftentimes the result of data being breached. On average, a single data breach for an organization will cost around $3 million, through lost revenue, a negative public reputation, and most importantly, loss of customers’ reliability on the company. This means that cybersecurity is something that businesses cannot afford to ignore, and should aim to educate their staff on the matter and implement measures to prevent future attacks.

It is estimated that the average business will invest between 6 to 14% of their yearly IT budget onto cybersecurity, which is around $20M to $50M per year for a large scale company. In turn, there is a growing trend in the amount of job positions being created in the cybersecurity industry, as there is an ever increasing need for professionals. Paired with most companies willing to spend generously to protect data, many cybersecurity jobs are high-paying, with most positions going above $100k a year.

Blockchain/Cryptocurrencies

What does it do?

Since the introduction of Bitcoin in 2008, blockchains and cryptocurrencies have been quick on the rise to popularity. Since then, many more coins/tokens were made, with some popular ones being: Ether (ETH), XRP, Cardano (ADA) and some branded as “memecoins” such as Dogecoin, which is based on the “Doge” meme circulating back to early 2010s, which consists of a Shiba Inu dog accompanied by random text. With many more to come, it is good to understand what they mean for society today, and possible effects it will have on our daily lives.

A block is a collection of data/records stored digitally. Hence, a blockchain is a chain of multiple blocks that stores information electronically in a digital format. Blockchains are the technology that essentially enable cryptocurrencies to exist. The innovative aspect with blockchains is that they assure the security of a record of data without a need for a central clearing authority. They differ from databases in the way the data is structured, blockchains are decentralized and collect data in the form of blocks and link them together. On the other hand, databases are often centralized, where a central authority handles the actions in the database and structures their data in the form of tables. While this way can be quicker in most cases, it is ultimately more costly and lacks the security that blockchains have.

Cryptocurrency, in its simplest form is digital money that is bought and sold online, no need for traditional paper bills or coins, it is not based on a physical asset such as gold and silver, and it doesn’t go through financial institutions like banks. Instead, cryptocurrencies operate in a fully decentralized system that uses blockchain technology to track transactions. Some benefits of using cryptocurrency as a payment method is that it is free from any third party interference, which is why crypto transactions never fail. Moreover, it is very easy to use it, through signing up for a digital wallet of crypto and start buying and trading straight away. Cryptocurrency can not only be bought but also “mined”. In the context of Bitcoin, mining is done by advanced computer systems that try to solve a difficult computational math problem, afterwards, the first computer to solve it will receive the next block of bitcoin.

As of 2020, the global cryptocurrency market is worth at USD$1.44 billion and with the COVID-19 pandemic in place, people stuck at home have found their way onto crypto trading for the very first time in hopes of making a fortune at this perilous time period. However, as quickly as an individual can strike gold, it can be as quick to go bankrupt.

A notable cryptocurrency scam that has been trending as of lately are “rug pulls”, which is when a crypto project (usually a coin or token) is heavily promoted/pumped up in value before the developers abandon the project and essentially run away with everyone’s money, leaving the coin with little to no value. This type of scam was responsible for over $3 billion lost for victims in 2021 according to Chainanalysis, a blockchain platform.

An example of a fairly recent rug pull is the “Squid Game” crypto coin, which is based on the Netflix South Korean TV Series. Using the hype at the time to their advantage, developers of the Squid coin rushed to promote their project which in turn soared in value as thousands of investors bought tokens without much thought. The coin was valued at around $2,800 at its peak, an increase of 23million percent, but all came crashing down to less than less than 1 cent in a span of a few minutes. Numerous investors lost their hard-earned cash as the project was ditched by the developers and ran away with millions of dollars.

What is the likely impact?

Cryptocurrency has had a severe impact on global relations and the general economy of the world. It is safe to say that it has steadily established itself as a feasible currency and form of investment, but is not ready to be accepted by most people. As of 2022, there are over 15,000 cryptocurrencies in existence, and a little over 100 million people in the world own some form of crypto.

There is no doubt that cryptocurrency is widely recognised at a global level and can either pose an opportunity for wealth or a threat for certain companies and even countries. Nine countries (Egypt, Iraq, Qatar, Oman, Morocco, Algeria, Tunisia, Bangladesh, and China) currently have a complete ban on the use of cryptocurrency, along with 42 others who placed implicit bans on the idea. The main reasoning behind this is that, it is said that crypto can be used in a variety of illegal ways such as: money laundering, fraud, drug trafficking, human trafficking, child exploitation, and funding criminal organisations. Criminals favor crypto over other forms of payment because while transactions on the blockchain are public record, the identities of those people who make the transaction remain unknown and therefore harder to track by government authorities.

However, putting aside the possible drawbacks of cryptocurrency, the benefits far outweigh them. Blockchain technology has proven to improve economies in a number of ways according to Pelicoin.com:

  • Blockchain has improved financial institutions’ cross-border transactions.
  • Cloud computing can use Blockchain to execute smart contracts and resist hacking.
  • Government and public records can use Blockchain to reduce paperwork and fraud while increasing accountability.

All in all, cryptocurrency generally has had a positive impact globally, allowing everyone regardless of situation to freely trade internationally, thus creating an “economic equality”.

I have gotten into trading crypto around the start of 2021, starting off with serious projects such as Bitcoin and Ethereum, making small profits through day trading. In April of 2021, thanks to hype from Tesla founder, Elon Musk, the price of Dogecoin surged 400% in just one week. At the time, making it top 5 for highest-valued cryptocurrencies. A little over a month later, the coin passed the 50c mark, which marks over a 20,000% increase in just one year.

Cloud, Services, Servers

What does it do?

Cloud services allow for the remote use of computational power rather than using a local serveror personal computer. The state of the art of this technology is the ability to be able to usepowerful resources without physically owing them. Instead of purchasing and maintaining aserver on your own with capabilities that you may or may not need, instead you pay for a servicethat suits your needs specifically, If you suddenly require more computing power or morestorage, you ask and pay for more, or if you don’t need as much power or storage you can savemoney and pay for less. In comparison to a local server, if these issues arise it can be moreproblematic changing hardware and performing upgrades/downgrades for yourself, unless youknow exactly what you’re doing.

As of today's technology cloud services can be utilised toperform the same tasks that local servers and personal computers can perform, except they areremote and are owned by exterior companies, examples of use cases are data backups, disasterrecovery, email, virtual desktops, software development and testing, big data analytics, andcustomer-facing web applications. These are the basic and popular uses for the use of theseservices. Some of the companies that provide these services include: Microsoft Azure, AmazonWeb Services(AWS), Google Cloud, IBM Cloud and Oracle Cloud Infrastructure. Anotherexample is Google Stadia, which takes advantage of cloud computing in order to allow users toplay games without the need for a powerful computer or dedicated console to play it on.

Features that they are advertising is for users to be able to play games anywhere where they have astable internet connection, whether it be on a phone, tablet or tv. They also are boasting thatusers will never need to download anything, no game downloads or updates. Cloud computing isutilised here to access the required hardware and software to run the video games and streamthe output to users on their device. The technology that is used for services such as GoogleStadia will continue to advance and in the near future companies may not even own personalcomputers. For example at RMIT cloud services are used to complete large workloads onsubjects such as genomic sequencing that would be usually too large to run on regular servers.

In the near future, it is likely to see cloud services improve by allowing more storage that will becheaper and faster, this also relies on the ever improving storage technology that is becomingfaster, cheaper, more reliable and secure. It will also improve as IoT does as they complimenteach other greatly, with cloud computing allowing the IoT a centralised server that containsresources that can be utilised whenever needed which allows for real time control and datamonitoring. With the help from other rapidly advancing technologies, Gartner predicts that by2026 public cloud spending will exceed 45% of all enterprise IT spending, up from less than 17%in 2021, directly demonstrating the ability of cloud and its ever growing uses for the future. Theadvance in other technologies such as wireless communications will allow for the cloud to beused in a broader way, enhancing the experience of some use cases such as mobile bankingand healthcare transformation, making these services becoming more used in everyday life fornot just people who work in IT but everyone that uses technology.

Robots

What does it do?

There are 5 types of robots they come in all shapes and sizes to efficiently carry out the task forwhich they are designed to do.

Pre-programmed robots

Pre-programmed robots operate in a controlled environment where they do simple,monotonous tasks. A great example of pre-programmed robots is mechanical arms onan automotive assembly line putting cars together. The robotic arm serves one functionranging from welding a door on, inserting a certain part into the engine, or even carryingcars to another position. The reason for the pre-programmed to serve one function is that it will perform the task for a much longer period as well as being faster, consistent,and therefore efficient in comparison to a human.

Humanoid robots

humanoid robots are robots that look can have the potential to mimic human behavior.These robots usually perform human-like activities such as jumping, running, or carryingobjects, and are sometimes designed aesthetically to look like humans, by doing so, therobots will sometimes have human faces and expressions. Examples of humanoidrobots in today's society are the prominent video of ‘Hansons Robtoics’ Sophia’ wherethey have engineered and created a robotic head with a human face that is able toexpress feelings based on the user's verbal interaction with ‘Sophia’.

Autonomous robots

Autonomous robots operate independently of human operators. These robots areusually created with the intention to carry out tasks in open environments that do notrequire human supervision or control. Autonomous robots are different in comparison toother robots as they operate with sensors around the body to perceive the world aroundthem, they also have employed decision-making structures controlled by a computer totake the optimal next step based on their data and intentions. An example ofautonomous robots that live in today's society are cleaning bots such as ‘Roombavacuum cleaners, which use sensors to roam freely throughout a home, anotherexample of these robots are autonomous drones that are able to continue flying andmove around on their own within a targeted location.

Teleoperated robots

Teleoperated robots are semi-autonomous bots that are used on a wireless network toallow humans to control from a safe distance. These robots are usually seen to work inextreme geographical weather, conditions, and circumstances. There are numerousexamples of teleoperated robots being used around our world, for example, these robots are able to fix underwater pipe leaks during an oil spill by a human-controlled submarine, another prime example are the drones used by the military to detect landmines on abattlefield.

Augmented Robots

Augmented robots either enhance the current human capabilities or replace thecapabilities a human may have lost. Although this field of robots has yet to unravel thetrue potential of enhancement. Scientists are hypothesizing that bots have the ability toredefine the definition of humanity by making humans faster and stronger. There aresome examples of current augmented robots such as prosthetic limbs or basicexoskeletons that are utilised to lift heavy weights or carry heavy objects from A to B.

Robots are all around us and we unintentionally interact with them despite us realising it is evena robot to be with, as we have made robots to talk to us, listen to us, perform as asked, andeven solve problems without specific direction from humans. For example, siri or any sort ofdevice A.I that interacts with us is considered being a robot, for that instance, we are evolving tocreate robots that are responsive with the help of artificial intelligence to create a full physicalinteraction between us and the robot. The most common use for robots involves them being analternative for human labor, doing work that humans could do but doing it better as they are notfragile in comparison to humans.
